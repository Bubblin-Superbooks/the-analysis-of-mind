Hume's criterion fails in its original form. Macbeth speaks of—

         that suggestion
     Whose horrid image doth unfix my hair

And make my seated heart knock at my ribs Against the use of nature. The whistle of a steam-engine could hardly have a stronger effect than this. A very intense emotion will often bring with it—especially where some future action or some undecided issue is involved—powerful compelling images which may determine the whole course of life, sweeping aside all contrary solicitations to the will by their capacity for exclusively possessing the mind. And in all cases where images, originally recognized as such, gradually pass into hallucinations, there must be just that "force or liveliness"
